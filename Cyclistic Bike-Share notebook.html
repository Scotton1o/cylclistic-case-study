What is Cyclistic?

Cyclistic is a fictional bicycle ride share company in Chicago chosen for this case study. In 2016, Cyclistic launched a successful bike share offering. Since then, the program has grown to a fleet of 5824 bicycles that our geo-tracked and locked into a network of 692 stations across Chicago. The bikes can be unlocked from one station and return to any other station in the system anytime. Three options for passes customers may purchase are:

  1. Single-ride passes
  2. Full-day passes
  3. Annual memberships

Customers who purchase single-ride or full-day passes are referred to as casual riders. Customers who purchase annual memberships are referred to as Cyclistic members.

Cyclistics finance analysts have concluded that annual members are much more profitable than casual riders. Although the pricing flexibility helps Cyclistic attract more customers, Director of Marketing: Lily Moreno, believes that maximizing the number of annual members will be key to future growth. Rather than creating a marketing campaign that targets all new customers, Moreno believes there is a solid opportunity to convert casual riders into members. She notes that casual riders are already aware of the Cyclistic program and have chosen Cyclistic for their mobility needs.

Moreno has set a clear goal: design marketing strategies aimed at converting casual riders into annual members. In-order to do that, however, the team needs to better understand how annual members and casual riders differ, why casual riders would buy a membership, and how digital media would affect their marketing tactics. Moreno and her team are interested in analyzing the cyclic historical bike trip data to identify trends.

Three questions will guide the future marketing program:

  1. How do annual members and casual riders use Cyclistic bikes differently?
  2. Why would casual riders buy Cyclistic annual memberships?
  3. How can Cyclistic use digital media to influence casual riders to become members?

Average ride length.

In SQL calculate ride_length then calculate average ride_length grouped by member_casual and grouped by month

-- Step A: Add the new column
ALTER TABLE `temptables-465123.case_study.cyclistic_2025`
ADD COLUMN ride_length INT64;

-- Step B: Populate with the difference in seconds
UPDATE `temptables-465123.case_study.cyclistic_2025`
SET ride_length = TIMESTAMP_DIFF(ended_at, started_at, SECOND)
WHERE started_at IS NOT NULL AND ended_at IS NOT NULL;

SELECT 
    member_casual, 
    EXTRACT(MONTH FROM started_at) AS ride_month,
    AVG(ride_length) AS avg_ride_length_seconds
FROM `temptables-465123.case_study.cyclistic_2025`
WHERE ride_length IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY 
    member_casual, 
    ride_month
ORDER BY 
    ride_month, 
    member_casual;

Top ten start stations

Provide data on top ten start_station_name for casual riders. This sql code gives Top 10 start_station_name for casual riders on day_of_week Saturday and Sunday during months of June, July, Aug. Without including Null values

SELECT 
    start_station_name, 
    COUNT(ride_id) AS ride_count
FROM temptables-465123.case_study.cyclistic_2025
WHERE 
    -- Filter for casual riders
    member_casual = 'casual'
    -- Filter for specific months (6=June, 7=July, 8=August)
    AND EXTRACT(MONTH FROM started_at) IN (6, 7, 8)
    -- Filter for weekend days
    AND day_of_week IN ('Saturday', 'Sunday')
    -- Exclude null values from the grouping column and ID column
    AND start_station_name IS NOT NULL
    AND ride_id IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY 
    start_station_name
ORDER BY 
    ride_count DESC
LIMIT 10;

SQL code to show top ten start_station_name, start_station_id, start_lat and start_lng used by casual-riders on Saturday and Sunday during months June, July, and August. This will be used to build a geographic visual in Tableau.

SELECT 
    start_station_name, 
    start_station_id, 
    start_lat, 
    start_lng, 
    COUNT(ride_id) AS total_rides
FROM `temptables-465123.case_study.cyclistic_2025`
WHERE 
    -- Filter for casual riders only
    member_casual = 'casual'
    -- Filter for June (6), July (7), and August (8)
    AND EXTRACT(MONTH FROM started_at) IN (6, 7, 8)
    -- Filter for weekends (1 = Sunday, 7 = Saturday in BigQuery)
    AND EXTRACT(DAYOFWEEK FROM started_at) IN (1, 7)
    -- Exclude null station data to ensure clean results
    AND start_station_name IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY 
    start_station_name, 
    start_station_id, 
    start_lat, 
    start_lng
ORDER BY 
    total_rides DESC

What story does this data tell us?

Rider Base and Conversion Potential

Annual members currently outnumber casual riders by 28%. This variance highlights a significant opportunity to develop targeted campaigns aimed at converting the casual rider segment into annual members.

Rideable Type Preferences

Both rider demographics demonstrate a preference for electric bikes. Marketing strategies should prioritize ensuring high availability and prominent promotion of the electric fleet.

Usage Patterns and Timing

Casual riders generally take longer average trips and exhibit significantly higher maximum ride lengths specifically during March and June. The sole periods where casual riders surpass members in ride count are on Saturdays and Sundays in the summer months (June, July, August). The optimal time to market to this demographic is explicitly during summer weekends.

Location-Based Targeting

Popular start station locations differ markedly between groups. Casual riders favor stations along the eastern lakeshore. Annual members prefer stations within the inner city and southern areas near Hyde Park. The most effective location-based marketing campaigns should be deployed along the eastern lakeshore to capture casual rider attention.
